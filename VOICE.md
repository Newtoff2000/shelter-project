# Voice & Tone — Ericeira Paws Website

> This document defines how the website speaks. It covers the register, the persona, specific language decisions, CTA copy, and how the website voice relates to (but differs from) the existing Instagram/Facebook voice. Companion to [UX.md](./UX.md) and [SHELTER.md](./SHELTER.md).

---

## 1. The Existing Social Media Voice (what we're aligning with)

Before defining the website voice, here's what we're working from. The Instagram and GoFundMe voice has these characteristics:

| Trait | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Direct, simple English** | Written by non-native speakers; clarity over polish: "We are a group of volunteers coming from different parts of the world" |
| **Emoji as punctuation** | ❤️ ✨ 💙 🙏🏻 used liberally in bios, captions, and story titles |
| **Earnest and unguarded** | No irony, no cleverness for its own sake: "Today we give our time and energy to those animals so that they could see a little bit of light and nature" |
| **First-person plural** | "We", "our", "us" — the team speaks as a group |
| **Sometimes animal first-person** | "Bring me home!" — the animal speaking through the caption |
| **Direct asks** | "Donate if you can 👇🏻", "we NEED YOUR HELP" |
| **Transparency as identity** | "We will be totally transparent with you, and we will share all information" |
| **Celebration on success** | "✨ADOPTED✨" — all caps, sparkle emoji, joyful |
| **Affectionate animal naming** | "Odin boy", "Beautiful young..." — warm, personal |

**What works about this voice:** It's genuine. It's the voice of people who actually show up at 9am on a Monday to walk dogs in a public shelter. It doesn't sound like a marketing team. That authenticity is the brand's strongest asset.

**What the website should keep:** The warmth, the directness, the "we" voice, the transparency, the celebration of adoptions, the treatment of animals as individuals with names and personalities.

**What the website should change:** The register. A website isn't a feed. It needs to be steadier, more composed, less urgent. Instagram posts are moments — a post about a specific dog on a specific day. The website is a standing invitation. It should feel like walking into a warm room, not like being handed a flyer.

## 2. The Website Voice — Core Position

**The website speaks like a calm, warm friend who's been volunteering at the shelter for a year and is telling you about it over coffee.**

Not a fundraising pitch. Not a charity brochure. Not an Instagram caption.

This person:
- Loves the animals and knows them by name
- Doesn't guilt you — they inspire you
- Is honest about the conditions without being traumatic
- Makes the ask feel like an invitation, not a plea
- Speaks with quiet confidence: they know the work matters

### Voice spectrum

```
Pleading ←──────────────────────────────→ Corporate
         "Please help us 🙏"    "Submit your enquiry"
                        │
                        ▼
              ┌─── OUR VOICE ───┐
              │  Warm, direct,  │
              │  inviting,      │
              │  quietly proud  │
              └─────────────────┘
```

### Three words that define the voice

| Word | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| **Warm** | Animals are people you haven't met yet, not cases. Copy reads like it was written by someone who knows every dog's favourite spot to be scratched. |
| **Direct** | Short sentences. No filler. No "at Ericeira Paws, we believe that every animal deserves..." — just say what's true. The GoFundMe voice is already direct; the website should be even more so. |
| **Inviting** | Every piece of copy should make the reader feel welcome, not pressured. "Come meet them" not "They need you." The difference between a door that's open and a hand that's pulling. |

## 3. Key Voice Decisions

These are the specific, non-obvious choices that shape how the website actually reads. Each one has been decided, with reasoning.

### Decision 1: Who speaks on animal profiles?

**Chosen: Third-person, written as if by someone who knows the animal well.**

Not first-person ("I love belly rubs") — that's a device that works in Instagram captions but reads as contrived on a website profile page. Not clinical third-person ("This animal is 3 years old and neutered") — that's a database, not a personality.

Instead: the voice of someone introducing you to a friend.

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---|---|
| "Hi! I'm Luna and I love cuddles!" | "Luna will lean against your leg the moment you sit down. She's been doing it with every volunteer since the day she arrived." |
| "Luna is a 3-year-old female dog, medium-sized, neutered." | "Luna, 3 years old. Medium-sized, neutered. At the shelter since March 2025." |

The personality section is warm and narrative. The facts section is clean and scannable. These are two different registers on the same page, and that's fine — it mirrors how you'd actually describe a dog to someone: "She's wonderful, you'd love her" followed by "she's about knee-height, spayed, up to date on shots."

### Decision 2: How do we talk about the shelter conditions?

**Chosen: Honest but forward-looking. Acknowledge the reality, then immediately point to what's being done and what the visitor can do about it.**

The GoFundMe is explicit about suffering: "small boxes, constant noise, bad smell... wet and dirty wood palettes." This works for fundraising — it creates urgency. But the website has a different job. People adopt from hope, not guilt. The website should be honest about why these animals need homes without making the visitor feel helpless or distressed.

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---|---|
| "These animals live in terrible conditions, in small dirty boxes, with no outdoor time." | "The Mafra shelter houses around 50 animals with limited space and resources. Our volunteers give each one outdoor time, better food, and something they don't get enough of — attention." |
| "We NEED YOUR HELP" | "Every bit of help changes their week." |

**The principle:** describe the situation once, clearly and without exaggeration. Then spend all the remaining energy on the solution — what volunteers do, what donations buy, what adoption means. The emotional engine of the website is the possibility of a better life, not the documentation of a bad one.

### Decision 3: CTA language

**Chosen: Inviting and specific, not cute and not generic.**

CTA copy is the most consequential language on the site. Every button is a micro-moment where someone decides whether to act. The language must feel like a natural next step, not a commitment, and not a gimmick.

#### Primary CTAs

| Context | CTA text | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Hero (main page) | **"Meet our animals"** | Inviting. Low commitment. "Meet" implies a visit, not a contract. "Our" establishes that these are known, loved individuals. |
| Animal profile | **"I'd like to adopt [Name]"** | Personal — uses the animal's name. "I'd like to" is softer than "Apply to adopt" and more intentional than "I'm interested." |
| Foster section | **"I'd like to foster"** | Parallel structure with adopt CTA. Clear about what you're signing up for. |
| Donate | **"Donate"** | No cleverness needed. Donors know what they're doing. The surrounding copy does the emotional work; the button just needs to be findable and clear. |
| Volunteer | **"Join the team"** | Community-first language. Not "Sign up to volunteer" (transactional) or "Help us" (plea). "Join" implies belonging. |

#### Why not the alternatives

| Rejected option | Why |
|---|---|
| "Browse our dogs" | Reduces animals to items in a catalogue. "Browse" is for shops. |
| "Cute paws here" | Too cutesy — undermines the seriousness of the cause. Works in a sticker, not a CTA. |
| "Take me home" | First-person animal voice in a button is jarring. Also presumes a decision the user hasn't made yet. |
| "Start your adoption journey" | Corporate charity-speak. No one says "journey" in real life. |
| "Make a friend" | Close, but too vague — could mean anything. Doesn't tell the user what clicking the button actually does. |
| "Help us" | Plea framing. The website voice invites, it doesn't plead. |
| "Submit enquiry" | Dead bureaucratic tone. Kills the emotional momentum of the animal profile page. |
| "Apply now" | Implies a process, gatekeeping, possible rejection. Adoption at this shelter is a conversation, not an application. |

### Decision 4: Emoji on the website

**Chosen: Almost none. Emoji in social shares and OG descriptions only.**

Instagram uses ❤️ ✨ 💙 🙏🏻 freely, and that's right for Instagram. The website should not. The warmth comes from the words and the photos, not from emoji. A website full of emoji reads as unfinished or amateur — and Ericeira Paws deserves better than that.

**Exceptions:**
- OG meta descriptions (seen in link previews on WhatsApp/iMessage) can use one emoji — it helps the preview stand out in a chat thread
- The success/adoption badge could use ✨ or 🏠 as a visual marker, but as an icon element, not inline text

### Decision 5: How do we handle bilingual tone?

**Chosen: The Portuguese voice is slightly warmer and more informal than the English voice. Both are genuine.**

Portuguese has natural warmth that English has to work harder for — diminutives, softer phrasing, a cultural tendency toward directness-with-affection. The English version shouldn't try to replicate this literally (that sounds like a bad translation). Instead:

- **PT-PT:** Can be slightly more informal, more conversational, can use colloquial warmth. "Vem conhecê-los" (come meet them) feels natural in Portuguese.
- **EN:** Slightly more composed, but still warm. "Meet our animals" — not stiff, just the English way of being inviting.

**The test:** Read each version aloud. If the PT version sounds like a charity brochure, it's too formal. If the EN version sounds like a translated Instagram caption, it's too informal. Each language should sound like it was written natively in that language.

### Decision 6: How do we celebrate adoptions?

**Chosen: Joyful but not shouty. The website version of ✨ADOPTED✨.**

Instagram uses all-caps sparkle emoji to celebrate adoptions, and it works there — it's a moment of public joy in a feed. The website equivalent needs to carry the same energy with more poise, since the adoption badge lives permanently on the profile page.

| Context | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Animal card in feed | A warm badge: "Found their home" with a subtle visual marker (soft colour, small icon) |
| Animal profile page | Status line: "Luna found her home in March 2025" — a sentence, not a label |
| Success Stories section | Full story: name, photo, brief narrative, date. "Odin spent four months at the shelter. Now he sleeps on a couch in Ericeira and goes to the beach every morning." |

**Not:** "ADOPTED! 🎉" — that's Instagram's register, not the website's.
**Not:** "Status: Adopted" — that's a database field, not a celebration.

## 4. Copy Patterns (reusable structures)

### The animal profile intro pattern

```
[Name], [age]. [One sentence that reveals personality — a specific behaviour,
a habit, a quirk that a volunteer has actually observed.]
```

Examples:
- "Benny, 4 years old. He brings his blanket to the door every time he hears footsteps."
- "Mia, 2 years old. She's the quietest dog in the shelter — until someone picks up a ball."
- "Tigre, 7 years old. He's been here longer than most of the volunteers. He still greets every single person like they might be the one."

**Why this pattern works:** It leads with the name (identity), gives the age (practical), then earns the emotional connection through a specific, observed detail. Specific beats generic. "Loves cuddles" could describe any dog. "Brings his blanket to the door" is one dog.

### The "what happens next" pattern

```
1. [Simple human action] — [one line of reassurance]
2. [Simple human action] — [one line of reassurance]
3. [Simple human action] — [one line of reassurance]
```

Example (adoption):
```
1. We'll get in touch — usually within a day or two.
2. You visit the shelter — meet them in person, take your time.
3. You take them home — we'll help with everything you need.
```

Example (fostering):
```
1. Tell us a bit about your situation — housing, schedule, experience.
2. We match you with an animal — based on what works for both of you.
3. You take them home — and we stay with you every step of the way.
```

**Why this pattern works:** Three steps is manageable. Each step starts with what the user does (agency), not what the organisation does (process). The reassurance line after each step reduces anxiety.

### The impact statement pattern

```
€[amount] — [specific, concrete thing it buys] — [for how long or for how many]
```

Examples:
- "€20 — a month of quality food — for one dog"
- "€50 — a vet check-up — for a dog in foster care"
- "€10 — vitamins and supplements — for a week"

**Why this pattern works:** Concrete, scannable, no filler. The donor can see exactly what their money does. This is Ericeira Paws' transparency promise in action.

## 5. Words We Use / Words We Don't

| ✅ Use | ❌ Avoid | Why |
|---|---|---|
| animals, dogs, cats | pets | They're not pets yet — they're animals looking for a home. "Pet" implies ownership that hasn't happened. |
| home, family | forever home | "Forever home" is shelter-industry jargon that's become cliché. "Home" is enough. |
| found their home | adopted (as a badge) | Warmer, more narrative. "Adopted" is a status; "found their home" is a story. |
| the shelter, Mafra shelter | the facility, the centre | "Shelter" is honest and human. "Facility" and "centre" are euphemisms. |
| volunteer, team member | staff | There is no staff. Everyone is a volunteer. Using "staff" misrepresents the organisation. |
| help, support | save, rescue | "Save" and "rescue" imply the animal is in danger right now. Some are; most are in a difficult but stable situation. Overstating urgency erodes trust. Use "save/rescue" only for genuinely urgent cases. |
| join the team | sign up | "Join" implies community. "Sign up" implies a transaction. |
| give, donate | contribute | "Contribute" is formal and vague. "Give" and "donate" are direct. |
| we, our, us | Ericeira Paws (repeatedly) | First-person plural is warmer and matches the social voice. Use the organisation name when needed for clarity, but default to "we." |
| you | visitors, users, supporters | Address the reader directly. Always. |

## 6. Voice by Page

How the voice shifts depending on context — same personality, different register.

| Page | Register | Guiding emotion | Example line |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | Inviting, confident, brief | Hope | "They're waiting to meet you." |
| **Animal feed** | Minimal — the photos do the talking | Curiosity | Filter labels and animal names only. No paragraphs. |
| **Animal profile** | Warm, specific, personal | Connection | "Benny brings his blanket to the door every time he hears footsteps." |
| **Foster section** | Reassuring, honest, supportive | Confidence | "Even a few weeks gives them a break from the shelter — and gives you something real." |
| **Volunteer section** | Community-oriented, practical | Belonging | "There's a place for everyone. Some of us walk dogs. Some of us make reels. All of us show up." |
| **Donate section** | Direct, transparent, concrete | Trust | "€20 feeds a dog for a month. Here's what we bought last month." |
| **Success Stories** | Joyful, narrative, proud | Pride | "Odin spent four months at the shelter. Now he sleeps on a couch in Ericeira." |
| **Contact form** | Simple, welcoming | Ease | "Tell us what you're thinking. We'll get back to you." |
| **"What happens next?"** | Clear, calm, step-by-step | Reassurance | "We'll be in touch within a day or two." |

## 8. Voice Don'ts — The Short List

1. **Don't use puns.** "Paws-itive," "fur-ever," "paw-some" — these are everywhere in shelter marketing and they undermine the seriousness of the cause. Ericeira Paws' name already has "paws" in it. That's enough.

2. **Don't guilt.** "These animals are suffering and you can help" is a guilt frame. "These animals are incredible and they're waiting for you" is a hope frame. Both are true. The second one converts better and feels better for everyone.

3. **Don't over-explain.** The Instagram voice is already direct. The website should be even more so. If a sentence can lose three words without losing meaning, lose them.

4. **Don't use stock phrases.** "Making a difference," "giving back," "every animal deserves love" — these are filler. They feel like every other shelter website. Say something specific to these animals in this shelter instead.

5. **Don't write in English that sounds translated from Portuguese.** And don't write in Portuguese that sounds translated from English. Each language version should read as though it was written by someone thinking in that language. This may mean the PT and EN versions of a paragraph say slightly different things — that's fine. Equivalent meaning is better than equivalent words.

6. **Don't hide behind the organisation.** Say "we" not "the organisation." Say "you" not "potential adopters." This is a conversation between people who care about animals.
